Overview

Overview of the Labour Force Survey

The Labour Force Survey (LFS) is the most reliable source of Canadian labour market statistics. It provides up-to-date and historical data on employment status, including full-time, part-time, and self-employment, as well as unemployment. These categories can be further characterized by various demographic factors such as age, sex, marital status, education, and ethnicity. The LFS also provides important information regarding the distribution of labour across Canadian industries, the types of occupations that people have, the reasons why people leave work, and the level of labour unionization.

This being the case, the LFS is an important source of information for both government and research because:

  • increases in unemployment rate can be indicative of long-term structural economic issues that could require government intervention to resolve
  • the unemployment rate puts pressure on the federal budget, as due to the costs of employment insurance
  • in research, the survey can be a source of analysis for socioeconomic phenomena such as the gendered pay gap or the precarity and conditions of work for low-income workers, etc.

What is the Labour Force?

On the face of it, the term labour force appears to be quite simple. When someone pictures the labour force, they probably imagine folks with hard helmets working at a construction site, or people who are clacking away at computers in an office. Rarely does this picture also include people who have been recently laid-off, or people who are applying for new employment. This page clarifies what is meant by the term labour force in the Labour Force Survey (LFS), as well as other important but commonly misunderstood terms, such as employment, and full-time vs. part-time work.

Labour Force Survey Terms and Definitions

  • Labour Force
  • Employment
  • Unemployment
  • Full-Time vs. Part-Time

Generally in economics, the labour force is defined as the population of people who could be employed for work. This is different than the employed population, because it acknowledges that not everyone who would like to work has a job. That said, it does not include the entire population who is eligible for work. For example, full-time university students are eligible to work, yet many do not because of their time-commitment to their studies - because of this, they would not be considered a part of the labour force. Within the LFS, it is reasonable to think of the labour force according to the following equation:

Labour Force = Employed Population + Unemployed Population

 

The LFS defines employment as either:

  1. Those who in the past week have done some form of labour at a job or business, including self-employment and uncompensated work for a family business, or
  2. Those who in the past week have not done the above due to absence caused by illness, disability, personal/family responsibilities, vacation, or labour disputes.

So, employment is about a relationship between a person and an employer. If a person has done work for an employer recently, or was absent from work for some reason, then they are considered employed.

 

Unemployment captures those who are not working, but should still be considered a part of the labour force. In the LFS, unemployment is defined as those who in the past week were:

  • not working but were available for work, and
  • looking for work in the past four weeks (including the present one), or
  • laid off temporarily due to business conditions, or
  • had a job that they were starting in the next four weeks.

 

Everyone has a different idea of what classifies something as full-time or part-time employment. In the LFS,

  • full-time employment is where a person works at least 30 hours per week on average, and
  • part-time employment is when a person works less than 30 hours a week on average.

History

History of the Labour Force Survey

  • Origin: 1945
  • Redesign: 1960
  • Redsign: 1977
  • Redesign: 1997
  • Recent History

The Labour Force Survey (LFS) began as a quarterly survey in November of 1945, though it soon became a monthly survey in 1952, and remains so today. It was developed following the Second World War to facilitate a smooth transition of the national economy from a state of total-war measures to those suitable for modern industrial capitalism. The LFS at this time was designed to monitor changes in employment according to industry, for example, to observe changes in the number of workers employed in the forestry industry over the course of a year.

Prior to the LFS, labour trends could only be studied using the Canadian Census of Population and various employment services in Canada. The issue with these resources was their lack of frequency, use of out-dated questions, and inability to paint a holistic picture of who in Canada is employed and who is not.

In 1960, the need for reliable data regarding the Canadian unemployment rate made the LFS the official source of this statistic. Following this, the LFS also became the authority for all labour market statistics, requiring that it be expanded to include a broader range of data, especially at the regional level. Since then, the LFS has gone through many other redesigns to meet the demands of the present economic, social, and political landscape in Canada.

In 1977, the LFS questionnaire was reshaped to resemble how it looks today. This new questionnaire could paint a better picture of who was employed and who was unemployed, as it was more inclusive of youth and women. The LFS also began to collect information regarding hourly work, overtime, and education. Additionally, the definition of full-time work was modified from at least 35 hours of work per week to only 30 hours, considering the trend in Canada towards shorter working hours.

In 1997, the LFS questionnaire was again redesigned to become more sensitive to the labour market. Due to the proliferation of computers in government at this time, the survey was designed to make use of a computer’s ability to change question wording, response categories, and flow based on the interviewee’s previous responses. Additionally, the questionnaire was greatly restructured into well-defined components such as demographic information or labour force information. Category responses were also greatly modified during this redesign, especially regarding reasons for absence from work, becoming more sensitive to family care, pregnancy, and maternity.

Beyond these major redesigns to the LFS, in more recent years, questions have been added to the survey to identify Indigenous and immigrant respondents. This has allowed for examination of labour trends within these populations.

Target Population

A target population of a survey is the group of people who the survey is trying to collect data about. Not every survey should be given to everyone in the population, for a number of reasons, including the usefulness and feasibility of data collection from that population.

Target Population of the Labour Force Survey

  • Inclusions
  • Exclusions

Conceptually, the Labour Force Survey is an attempt to gather information about the state and features of Canada’s domestic labour force. Due to this, Statistics Canada defines the target population of the of LFS as individuals who are:

  • 15 years of age or older, and
  • living in Canada.

This definition corresponds to the concept above, as individuals aged 15 and older are, on average, the employable population in Canada – though the actual age of employment depends on provincial labour laws. Moreover, since the survey is concerned with domestic labour, it makes sense to only include those living in Canada, regardless of their citizenship.

Excluded from the target population of the Labour Force Survey are individuals who are:

  • living on reserves or other Aboriginal settlements in the provinces,
  • full-time members of the Canadian Armed Forces,
  • institutionalized, or
  • living in households in very remote areas with low population densities.

Statistics Canada justifies the exclusion of those living on reserves, members of the Canadian Armed Forces, and those living in remote areas of low population density, due to the practical difficulty and the costs associated with sampling and interviewing these populations.

Methodology

This page describes the manner in which the Labour Force Survey (LFS) is conducted, or in other terms, its methodology. The sampling methods at the provincial and territorial levels are discussed, as well as how data collection is conducted.

Provincial Sampling Methods

  • Census Redesign
  • Design Strata
  • Sampling
  • Interview Panels

Due to the need for the LFS to provide responsive data to changes in employment and unemployment in Canada, the LFS undergoes a redesign to its provincial sampling every ten years following a census. This redesign considers new population characteristics and changing geographic boundaries based on information from the census.

The information from the census is used to define new design strata every ten years. Put simply, this is a way that the population of Canada can be divided up into different geographic groups with similar labour characteristics. Moreover, some strata may be specialized to target specific populations, such as those with high incomes. By updating strata frequently, Statistics Canada can ensure that the population of each strata remains similar, and that their labour characteristics do not change too much.

The number of samples taken from each province and stratum with the provinces is determined so that the data meets a certain threshold of statistical reliability. The specific thresholds correspond to the national, provincial, and sub-provincial levels.

Sampling within provinces occurs in two stages. Within each stratum, clusters of approximately 230 households within a small geographic and continuous geographic area are defined.

  • For the first stage of sampling within a stratum, a group of about six clusters is sampled from every stratum.
  • At the second stage, the dwellings within the sampled clusters are then combined into a single list, and the final sample of dwellings is then selected from this list.

An exception to the two-stage sampling method described above is Prince Edward Island, which due to its characteristics, does not require the clustering stage. Instead, all dwellings within a given stratum are eligible to be sampled.

Once the sample of dwellings has been chosen, interviewers conduct the questionnaire with the members of the dwelling. Demographic information is collected from every member of the dwelling, while labour force information is only gathered from civilian members of the household who are at least 15 years of age, according to the target population.

This information is gathered monthly over a span of six consecutive months. At the end of this six-month period, to not overburden a single household, a new household in a similar area is selected to be sampled. The new households that join each month form what is called a panel. Panels are staggered so that only one panel leaves the sample group each time. Due to this, the month-to-month sample population is relatively stable, allowing for greater confidence when analyzing the changes in labour force characteristics that are captured by the survey.

Territorial Sampling Methods

  • Why Separate the Territories?
  • Community Stratification
  • Sampling
  • Exlcusion from National Totals

Due to the large geographic area and relatively small population of the territories, there are high costs involved in employing the same methods used within the provinces. In response to this, a distinct sampling method is employed within the territories to meet a similar threshold of statistical reliability while avoiding the frequency with which the provincial survey is conducted.

Unlike the provinces, the territories are stratified by community instead of labour characteristics. Within each territory, there are some communities that must be considered within every sample frame, making up their own stratum.

For example, in Nunavut: Iqaluit, Rankin Inlet, Cambridge Bay and Kugluktuk are always included in the sample frame. The remaining communities that are eligible for sampling are then assembled in groups of about 2-5, with one community selected from each of these groups as eligible to be sampled from. Like the provincial survey, the sample size of each stratum is determined by a threshold of statistical reliability.

Dwellings that are sampled from this frame are then surveyed in a similar manner to the provinces. The questionnaire used within the territories is almost identical to the provincial questionnaire, except for some differences due to regional differences. Otherwise, like the provinces, basic demographic data is collected from all individuals, while labour information is collected from civilians who are 15 years of age or older.

Unlike the provincial survey, data is collected from households within the sample every three months over the course of two years. Overall, each household is interviewed eight times. At the end of this two-year period, each household is replaced with another that is from the same or a similar community.

It should be noted that due to the differences in sampling methods, the territories are excluded from national totals, and instead form their own separate data series within Statistics Canada publications, based on their three-month moving average, rather than the monthly total provided by the provincial survey.

Data Collection

  • Interviews
  • Respondent Who Move In/Out of the Dwelling
  • Non-Responses

Data is collected by employees of Statistics Canada who have been trained to conduct the interview for the LFS. Often, a knowledgeable member of the household acts as a proxy for the other household members in the interview. This is done to avoid repeated interviews at the same household for a given month of the survey. Quite often, surveys are conducted over the telephone with the assistance of a computer, though interviewers may also visit households to conduct interviews in the field. In certain circumstances, households may be able to complete the monthly survey online.

If during the six-month period that a dwelling is within the sample, the members of its household move out and are replaced by another household, the new household becomes the source of information for the survey for the remainder of the six-month period.

About 10% of households do not respond to the survey. While interviewers are instructed to make all reasonable attempts to obtain interviews with members of eligible households, at times, a response may not be given from a household for that month. A non-responsive household remains within the sample until its six-month block is finished. To account for non-responsive households, the data collected from responding households in the same area are given a greater weight to compensate for the missing data.

Survey Topics

The Labour Force Survey (LFS) covers a number of topics both directly and indirectly related to the labour market, including labour force information, demographics, and rent.

Labour Force Information provides the essence of the labour force survey, it is what allows policy makers and researchers to include the most important features and trends of the labour market within their analyses.

Demographics provide information about the respondents that may be exogenous to their labour force status, allowing policy makers and researchers to analyze social trends within the Canadian labour market.

Rent provides information about the living situation of people who do not own their homes, such as the number of bedrooms, the cost of rent, and the inclusion of utilities, services, and household appliances in the cost of rent. Note that this is only included in the provincial survey.

The subtopics found in the labour force information and demographics topics are examined more closely below.

Labour Force Information

  • Industry and Occupation
  • Job Characteristics
  • Labour Force Status
  • Nature of Workplace
  • Unemployment

The main basic features of the respondent’s employment (if applicable), including the sector, industry, and occupation that they work within. For example, a public sector employee who works in the fishing, hunting, and trapping industry in an occupation of art, culture, recreation, and sport.

The characteristics of a respondent’s job (if applicable), including the permanency of their position, the length of their employment, the number of jobs they work, the amount of overtime they work, the amount of compensation and benefits they receive, and any absences they have recorded.

The respondents labour force status is recorded based on whether they have worked in the last week, and if not the reasons why. This is important to distinguish individuals who are unemployed versus those who are not participating in the labour force.

The nature of the respondent’s workplace (if applicable), including the size of their employer’s establishment and firm, and whether the employee is a part of a labour union.

The nature of the respondent’s unemployment (if applicable), including the duration of their unemployment, the nature of their job search, and their reasons for leaving previous employment.

Demographics

  • Basic Information
  • Economic Family
  • Education
  • Geography
  • Immigration
  • Indigeneity

The age, sex, and marital status of the respondents.

The respondent’s family’s economic structure; based on the parent’s labour force and marital status, as well as the age of their youngest child. For example, a dual-earner couple whose youngest child is aged between 0 and 17 years.

The levels of education attained by the respondents, such as secondary or post-secondary diplomas/degrees. Also, whether the respondent is currently a student.

The geographic location of the respondents at both provincial/territorial and sub-provincial/territorial levels.

The immigration status of the respondents, including their country of origin and their date of immigration (if applicable).

The Indigeneity of the respondent in relation to Canada, including whether they are First Nations, Métis, or Inuit (if applicable).

Data Products

After collecting the data from the Labour Force Survey (LFS) each month, Statistics Canada prepares several products that analyze this data in some meaningful way or reorganize and derive new data sets that are suitable for different levels of research. This section of the guide describes provides access to some of these important products in more detail, namely, reports & publications, aggregated data sets, public use microdata files, and master files. They are arranged in order of background knowledge in labour statistics required, from least to most.

Reports and Publications (most user friendly, no data knowledge required)

  • About
  • The Daily
  • Labour Force Information
  • Research Papers
  • Aboriginal & Immigrant Labour Force Analysis Series

These publications present information gathered from the LFS in a non-numerical format, often supported by helpful commentary, interpretation, and visualization (charts, graphs, etc.). The publications where are good starting place for those who are unsure of what information is considered important in the LFS, or those who are unfamiliar with data analysis.

The Daily is a news source published by Statistics Canada from Monday to Friday of every week of the year, with the exceptions of holidays. A monthly feature of The Daily is dedicated to summarizing the most notable trends of the latest LFS results. This publication is prepared by experts in labour market statistics, making it a very useful resource for those who are new to the subject and statistics.

  • The Daily Labour Force Survey release schedule

The most important report published from the LFS is the monthly Labour Force Information publication. This publication is released a month following each iteration of the LFS and contains charts that shows the three-year trends national and provincial labour market features, such as the unemployment, employment, and participation rates. This publication is useful to government and researchers because it provides easy and up-to-date access to the most important labour market indicators.

Note that in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic, the Labour Force Information publication was discontinued until further notice. Its most recent issue available presents data from February 2020.

  • Labour Force Information publication

While the above publications provide regular insights into the labour market in a succinct manner, at times there may be interesting trends in the labour market that cannot be given their due justice in these brief releases. Due to this, Statistics Canada releases less regular publication, called Research Papers, that take an in-depth examination of trends found in the Canadian labour market.

  • Labour Statistics: Research Papers

The series of publications in this section examine longer term (approximately five-year) labour market trends in the off-reserve Indigenous and the immigrant population in Canada. These publications feature data presented in charts as well as commentary to accompany them. They are released infrequently, with less than ten publications in each series thus far.

  • Aboriginal Labour Force Analysis
  • Immigrant Labour Force Analysis

Aggregated Data Sets (user friendly, familiarity with data required)

  • About
  • Canadian Labour Market Observatory
  • Data Visualizations
  • Data Tables

Aggregated data sets take the data collected from individuals in the LFS and combine them to produce new data at a level that is more meaningful, such as at the level of province or the entire nation. Often, these data sets allow for the user to explore different features and apply their own analyses, unlike reports and similar populations. That said, individual records from the data are excluded, prohibiting more in-depth analyses. These data sets are most useful for users who are new to labour statistics, but who cannot find the information to the level of specificity they require from the Statistics Canada reports.

This is a small bundle of labour market data sets paired with visualization applications that are built into the browser. The two products included in this bundle are the Labour Force Survey in brief and Earnings and payroll employment in brief. Each of these datasets allow the user to visually select the source region of their data, as well as well as other filters such as sex and industry. This is a useful tool to visually observe trends in specific regions without the immediate need for in-depth analysis.

  • Canadian Labour Market Observatory

Similar to the applications above, Statistics Canada provides another set of visualizations for aggregated labour market data called the Labour Market Indicators. The two publications differ by their use of seasonally adjusted and unadjusted data. Moreover, the seasonally adjusted data provides information at the federal, provincial, and census metropolitan area (i.e., large city and surrounding areas) level, while the unadjusted data is available at the federal, provincial, and economic region.

  • Statistics Canada: Labour Market Indicator (seasonally adjusted)
  • Statistics Canada: Labour Market Indicator (unadjusted for seasonality)

In addition to these visual tools, Statistics Canada releases several tables coming from the LFS on a monthly, annual, and occasional basis. Many of these summarize the labour force characteristics (i.e., the labour force size, employment by type, and unemployment) across various categories, such as students or immigrants. It is important to note as well that this is where the labour force characteristics for the territories by a three-month moving average can be found as well. There are other tables that can be found on Statistics Canada’s website, the user is encouraged to explore them to find out what is available.

  • Labour Force Survery Data Tables

Public Use Microdata Files (somewhat user friendly, data knowledge required)

  • About
  • Statistics Canada Microdata Files
  • odesi

Public use Microdata Files (PUMFs) are data sets that are disaggregated, that is, they keep individual data points without any grouping. Moreover, this data set is comprehensive in terms of the features that it includes. Due to this, users may produce their own aggregations of the data by manipulating it, allowing for one to examine the correlation of different variable and to produce other statistics. That said, use of PUMFs requires greater expertise on the part of the user due to the need for data manipulation. Moreover, to protect privacy, variables that could be identifying are either excluded from the data set or are re-classified to become less identifying. For instance, although the address of an individual’s dwelling is recorded within the survey, that person’s address is not made available – instead, something broader such as their province will be displayed.

Every month, Statistics Canada releases the last month’s public use data set in a raw form. These come in both a table and a text format. In this raw form, it is nearly impossible to interpret the data in any meaningful way – to understand the categories of the data and the values for each category, one must also use the codebook that accompanies the data. Often, the microdata and the codebook are used in tandem with a statistical software such as SAS, SPSS, STATA, or R. Using the microdata in this way can yield the best analyses due to the high-level of analysis enabled by this software.

  • Labour Force Survey: Public Use Microdata File

There are also browser-based tools that can be used to investigate the microdata in a more user-friendly way. These tools have already loaded the data and interpreted it according to the codebook so that users can immediately begin interpreting the data. Options within this software include the quick access to variable descriptions, the creation of tables, and the correlation analysis of variables. At Western, users are given access to odesi, an example of one of these tools.

  • Labour Force Survey @ odesi

Master Files (user unfriendly, data expertise required)

  • About
  • When You Should Use the Master Files
  • Research Data Centres
  • Real Time Remote Access

Master files are the closest that a user can get to the actual results from the survey. They contain microdata at a high level of specificity, allowing for the most in-depth analyses of all the products listed on this page. That said, their specificity makes them subject to a high degree of security to protect the identity of those whose data is found in these files. This security is such users are rarely granted access to the master files, and if they are, it is only under very strict parameters. The two main ways to utilize the master files of the LFS are through either a Research Data Centre (RDC) or through Real Time Remote Access (RTRA).

Access to the master files requires far more investment in obtaining access to them than in comparison to the PUMFs. Moreover, the usefulness of the data in master files is completely determined by the research that you are conducting. Prior to applying for access to the master files, researchers who are new to labour force statistics should use the LFS PUMFs to determine whether access to the master file is necessary. In many cases, the PUMF will be sufficient. If you are still uncertain, please contact your institution’s data librarian for their advice.

Research Data Centres (RDCs) are special facilities for access to master file data. They are generally found in either academic institutions or federal/provincial/territorial government buildings. Only government employees can be given access to this data. That said, researchers from outside of the government may apply to become an honorary employee of Statistics Canada, thereby granting them access to the master files.

  • Research Data Centre Information
  • Research Data Centre Locations
  • Research Data Centre Application

Real Time Remote Access (RTRA) grants researchers the ability to run SAS programs on the master file data set. In this way, the research is never given direct access to the data – instead, analyses can be carried out on the data and their results be shared with the researcher so that no one’s confidentiality is breached. Members of institutions with access to an RTRA can apply to become an RTRA member.

  • Real Time Remote Access Information
  • Real Time Remote Access Application

Data Granularity

The Labour Force Survey (LFS) contains 148 questions. Not all these questions are relevant to the labour market (some are more formal questions that ensure survey quality and repeatability), but even that said, much of the survey respondent’s answers to questions cannot be found in the available survey data itself. This page discusses how the data in Public Use Microdata Files contrasts with the data that is collected from the survey, namely the derived, collapsed, suppressed, and top-coded variables.

Data Granularity in the Labour Force Survey

  • Derived Variables
  • Collapsed Variables
  • Suppressed Variables
  • Top-coded Variables

Some of the most important questions that the LFS seeks to answer are formed in the data set by derived variables. A derived variable, for our purposes, is a data point that comes from multiple other questions in the survey.

For example, the labour force status. Due to the complexity and specificity of the definition of employment, it is not reliable to simply ask respondents what their labour force status is. Instead, questions such as “Did you work last week?” and “Are you available for work?” must be asked to distinguish the various labour force classifications from one another, according to a precise logic. For researchers, many of the responses to these questions are excluded, and only labour force status is shown.

This enforces Statistics Canada’s definition of employment upon the data set, making it more difficult to compare labour force status to other regions with incomparable definitions.

Often, survey respondents will be asked a question with a very specific answer, such as “How old are you?”. While this data is collected, the specificity of these questions can risk breaching confidentiality for respondents. Thus, for the purposes of privacy, some question responses that are technically different will be grouped together into courser categories.

For example, with the question regarding age, responses will be grouped into five-year non-overlapping intervals, such as 15-19 years or 20-24 years. Other examples of variables that are collapsed like this are industry, occupation, and immigration status.

Due to this, labour market analyses of specific industries, occupations, age groups, or immigrant cohorts are often not possible with the LFS PUMF.

Like collapsed variables, sometimes sharing any information at all about a particular variable may violate confidentiality. In cases such as these, the data from a respondent’s record is completely excluded entirely from the LFS PUMF or is present in a way that is incredibly coarse.

For example, questions regarding ethnicity and Indigeneity are suppressed from LFS PUMF to protect privacy. Likewise, respondents who live outside of the 9 most populated census metropolitan areas have their data essentially suppressed by categorizing them as “Other”.

As it is with collapsed variables, suppressed variables make it impossible to design some analyses that target variables whose data is suppressed.

Top-coded variables are are similar to collapsed and suppressed variables, in that they create coarseness in the data. Top-coding can happen in data that has a natural order, for example, ages (which are ordered from youngest to oldest). In top-coding, the largest values have a cap put on them.

For example, in the age variable of the FLS PUMF, the five-year categories are cut-off at the age of 70, at which point we are only given 70 years and older. Another example of this is hours worked. For this variable, values are capped at 99 hours per week.

In each case, these caps may be reasonable given that the labour force is mainly composed of people younger than 70, and that 99 hours worked in a week is an incredibly high amount. Nonetheless, it is important for researchers to know the parameters and limitations that have been placed on the variables found in the LFS PUMF, so that they can interpret them accordingly.

Recent Changes

The Labour Force Survey (LFS) constantly undergoes changes to provide the best information possible to government and researchers. This page collects some changes that the LFS has undergone in the past five years.

Recent Changes to the Labour Force Survey

  • Online Option (2015)
  • Elections Supplementary Questions (2016, 2019)
  • Questionnaire Redesign (2020)
  • Health and Safety Measures (2020)
  • COVID-19 Supplementary Questions (2020-2021)

In 2015, respondents to the LFS who met special criteria where able to complete the survey online following their first interview.

In years of federal elections, voter participation is studied by the addition of supplementary questions to the LFS during the cycle directly following the federal election.

In 2020, eight new questions were added to the LFS survey. These questions gather information about individuals with multiple jobs, employment benefits, the main activity of those not in the labour force, and more.

In the wake of COVID-19, to protect both interviewers and survey respondents, survey interviews that would have previously been conducted in-person were transitioned to telephone interviews and online survey response only.

Due to the massive impact of COVID-19 on the labour market in Canada (and globally), the LFS had a supplementary set of questions appended to it that could be answered on a voluntary basis by respondents. This set of questions changed overtime as the pandemic progressed – but these questions generally asked about the workplace health and safety as well as financial impacts of COVID-19.
